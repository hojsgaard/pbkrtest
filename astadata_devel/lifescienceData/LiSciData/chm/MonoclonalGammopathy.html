Details

The population in study was classified according to the age (from 30 to 70 years old), the type of life (farming and non-farming) and the gender (male and female).

A monoclonal gammopathy indicates the presence of an abnormal protein in your blood. The protein is produced by a group of cells in the bone marrow called plasma cells. Plasma cells are normally found in the bone marrow and represent approximately 1 percent of all marrow cells. They produce the antibodies that help your body fight infection.

Abnormal proteins circulating in the blood are not rare. They occur in 1 percent of healthy people over the age of 50 and in 3 percent of people over the age of 70.

Generally, the abnormal protein in the blood produces no symptoms. Most monoclonal gammopathies are detected incidentally as part of a complete physical examination.

This abnormal protein does not represent cancer. However, there is a 20 percent chance that the monoclonal gammopathy may develop into a more serious condition, including some forms of cancer.

No evidence suggests a substantial increase in risk for the spouse or immediate family members of someone with monoclonal gammopathy. They do not need to be checked for an abnormal protein if they are feeling well.
